01 — Problem

The Gap
in Design

"How can we make a variety of handled tools more accessible to people of reduced hand motor functions?"

Millions of people live with arthritis, Parkinson's disease, stroke-related weakness, and cerebral palsy — all conditions that significantly impact grip strength and dexterity.

Everyday tools are engineered around a single idealized hand grip. For those who cannot achieve that grip, the result is exclusion from activities most people take for granted.

This isn't a niche problem. It's a design failure at scale — and it demands a better answer.

02 — Solution

Five Systems,
One Device

A modular, wearable gripping aid built from five integrated subsystems — each validated through prototyping, testing, and real stakeholder feedback.

01
🧤

Compression Brace

An off-the-shelf compression wrist sleeve forms the foundation of the device. It fits securely without Velcro, supports impaired joints, and is fully washable — with sewn magnet pockets built into the palm.

02
🔵

Brace Magnet Array

Neodymium disc magnets are housed in sewn pockets on the palm of the brace. With each magnet rated at 5.5 lbs. of holding force, a three-magnet arrangement exceeds the 15 lb. load target while still releasing cleanly with a simple twist.

03
🔲

Handle Magnet Mount

A bar magnet is embedded into the 3D printed tool attachment using glue. It sits just above flush with the surface to snap securely to the brace magnets while remaining fully enclosed and protected.

04
🔧

Universal Tool Strap

A TPU-printed flexible plate with looping straps wraps around the handle of virtually any tool. The strap friction system holds up to 18 lbs. across handles from 0.5 to 2 inches in diameter, with no tools needed to attach or remove.

05
🫧

Adaptive Core Grip

A TPU cone printed with low-density gyroid infill mimics the behavior of foam — deforming to accept handles of varying shapes and snapping back to grip them securely. Works in tandem with the universal strap and fits a wide range of everyday tools.

✦
⚡

Twist-to-Release

Stakeholder testing confirmed that rotating the handle relative to the brace drastically reduces the force needed to separate the two — giving users with limited grip strength an intuitive, low-effort way to attach and detach tools independently.
